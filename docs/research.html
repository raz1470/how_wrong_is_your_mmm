How Wrong Is Your MMM?

A practitioner's guide to collinearity bias in Marketing Mix Models

1 · The Problem 2 · The Cause 3 · The Fix 4 · The Impact 5 · How It Works 6 · Questions

TL;DR

  • What this is. A guide to a hidden risk in Marketing Mix Models, or MMMs (statistical models that estimate how much each marketing channel contributes to sales). The output you actually use, elasticity (the modelled link between a £ of spend and the resulting change in sales), is the number ROI and budget-allocation decisions are built on.
  • What we're showing. Marketing channels are usually collinear, with spend rising and falling together because the same planning decisions drive them all. That doesn't make your MMM biased, but it makes its elasticity estimates unstable: refit the same model on a slightly different data window and the number can swing by a third or more.
  • How the analysis is built. Everything here is simulation-based: we generate many plausible versions of the same market (same true elasticities, different noise), fit the model to each, and measure how much the answers vary. The same method runs on your own spend data for a personalised result.
  • Why it's worth your time. If your elasticities swing by 30%+ between refits, the ROI and budget decisions built on them are sitting on mostly noise. This guide shows how to measure that risk, and a concrete fix, budget phasing, that reduces it without touching your annual budget.

Section 1

The Problem

Every MMM produces numbers: TV elasticity 0.42, Meta elasticity 0.28, Search elasticity 0.51. These numbers feed into budget recommendations, agency briefs, and board decks. The question most practitioners don't ask often enough is: how much should we trust them?

For most marketing datasets, the honest answer is less than you think, not because the model is broken, but because the data was never designed to answer the question.

Variance, not bias

This is the key technical point, and it matters for how you think about the problem. OLS (ordinary least squares, the standard regression method behind most MMMs) is still unbiased with correlated predictors. Given enough independent data, the average estimate would converge on the truth. The problem is that you don't have enough independent data, and you never will: 2–5 years of weekly observations, trying to identify 10–20 parameters in a world where channels have barely moved independently.

What you get instead is high variance: estimates that are centred near the truth on average, but swing widely in practice. Your specific TV elasticity (the number in your budget model) is one draw from a wide distribution. A company in the same market, with the same true elasticities, but a slightly different observation window, would get a different number.

Below: three channels, known true elasticities (TV 0.30, Meta 0.50, Search 0.40), mean pairwise correlation (the average correlation across every pair of channels) of 0.70. We simulate 50 plausible histories of the same market (same truth, different noise draws) and fit OLS to each one.

The coefficient of variation (standard deviation divided by mean estimate) measures how unstable the estimates are relative to their magnitude. In the simulation above (mean pairwise correlation 0.70), TV elasticity has a CV of 36%. That means if you refitted the same model on a slightly different data window, your number could shift by a third in either direction, not because the market changed, but because the data was never informative enough to pin it down.

The coefficient of variation, and how it scales with correlation

CV is a simple ratio: standard deviation of the estimated elasticity divided by its mean. A CV of 10% means estimates cluster tightly around their average: the model gives consistent answers across different data realisations. A CV of 40% means estimates swing wildly; the answer your specific dataset produced could easily be half or double the truth.

CV is more informative than a raw standard error (a measure of an estimate's precision, in the same units as the estimate itself) because it's relative to the magnitude of the estimate. A standard error of 0.05 means something very different if the elasticity is 0.10 versus 0.50. CV normalises this, making channels and models directly comparable.

Our simulations show this relationship between channel correlation and CV is not linear, it accelerates. Moving from correlation 0.3 to 0.5 roughly doubles TV elasticity CV. Moving from 0.5 to 0.9 more than doubles it again. At 0.9, the CV exceeds 50% for every channel, the model is producing more noise than signal.

At correlation 0.3 (CV 18%), estimates are noisy but directionally consistent. At 0.7 (CV 36%), a third of the estimate's magnitude is unexplained variance. At 0.9 (CV 60%), the model is largely guessing.

The practical inflection is around 0.6–0.7. Below that, the model is imprecise but still directionally useful: estimates are scattered but mostly on the right side of zero. Above 0.7, estimates become genuinely unreliable: the direction of the error is unpredictable, and the specific number your model produced is one of many equally defensible answers.

Most real marketing datasets sit between 0.5 and 0.8. That's the zone where collinearity is doing real damage, not obvious enough to flag, but severe enough to make budget decisions based on the output genuinely risky.

Getting a personalised number

Generic thresholds are useful benchmarks, but the most informative diagnostic uses your own spend data (your actual channel correlation structure) rather than a synthetic example.

The approach sidesteps the obvious problem: you don't know the true elasticities, which is the whole reason you ran the MMM. The diagnostic works around this by assigning plausible elasticities, simulating what sales would look like if those were true, then measuring how consistently OLS recovers them from your actual spend data. The collinearity (the thing driving unreliability) comes entirely from your real spend history. The sales simulation is just scaffolding to measure it.

The output is an elasticity interval per channel: not a point estimate, but a range. "Given how your channels actually moved over the last two years, your TV elasticity could plausibly be anywhere from X to Y." That range quantifies the unreliability in your specific data.

Your elasticity uncertainty report
80% interval across 50 noise seeds · based on real spend correlation structure (mean pairwise = 0.65) · diamond = mean estimate

A narrow interval means the channels varied independently enough for the model to distinguish their effects. A wide interval (especially one spanning more than the estimate itself) means the data design has failed: the number in your budget model is one of many equally plausible answers. Search typically shows the widest interval because it correlates most tightly with overall business activity.

Reading the output: if the 80% interval is narrower than 50% of the point estimate, the model is reasonably informative; wider than the estimate itself, treat it as directional at best. Search almost always shows wider intervals than TV or Meta, because search spend tracks overall business activity most tightly, treat a very wide Search interval as a rough order-of-magnitude indicator, not a number to optimise against. Run the diagnostic quarterly: widening intervals mean your spend has become more correlated over time; narrowing intervals mean the data design is improving.

Next: Section 2 — The Cause explains why this happens: channels that always move together can't be reliably told apart, however good the estimation method.

Section 2

The Cause

Marketing budgets are set by people, not algorithms. The same annual planning cycle controls spending across every channel simultaneously: when the demand forecast rises, TV, Meta and Search budgets all go up together. When a campaign launches, all channels activate at once. When there's a trading push, everyone gets funded.

The result is that week-to-week, channel spend patterns are highly correlated. TV is high when Meta is high. Meta is high when Search is high. They move together because the same business decisions drive them all.

Three channels, one planning cycle
Illustrative weekly spend · TV, Meta, Search · 52 weeks
TV Meta Search

All three channels follow the same underlying demand rhythm. When business conditions drive one channel up, they all go up, because the same planning cycle and demand signals control all of them.

This is not a modelling problem, it's a data design problem. Real marketing data is generated by business decisions, not experiments. And business decisions, by nature, are coordinated.

What this does to your MMM

An MMM estimates each channel's contribution by asking: "holding everything else constant, what does an extra pound of TV spend do to sales?" But if TV is always high when Meta is also high, the model never observes TV-high with Meta-low. It can't hold everything else constant, because nothing ever varies independently.

The result is that the model has to split the credit between channels that always moved together, and it does so inconsistently: which channel gets the credit can change a lot depending on small, essentially random differences in the data you happened to use. The elasticity estimates are unstable: small changes to the training window or the data vintage can shift the answer substantially.

Collinearity doesn't make the average estimate wrong. It makes any given estimate unreliable, sensitive to which period of data you used, which quarter you refitted, and what noise happened to turn up in your training window.

Why this matters more than you might think

Practitioners see this every time they refit. TV elasticity was 0.42 last quarter; now it's 0.31. The market hasn't changed. The model hasn't changed. Three more months of correlated data shifted the answer by 26%. That's high-variance estimation in practice, not a sign of a broken model, but a sign that the data never gave the model enough to work with.

Most teams respond by investigating the model: different priors, different transformations, different baseline specifications. This rarely helps, because the problem isn't the model. The problem is the data design. Channels that always moved together can't be reliably distinguished by any estimation method, however sophisticated.

The question isn't whether your MMM is biased. It probably isn't. The question is whether the data you gave it contained enough independent variation to pin down the channel effects, or whether the number it produced is just one of many equally plausible answers.

Next: Section 3 — The Fix shows how to deliberately reduce channel correlation in your spend plan, narrowing those intervals without touching monthly or annual budget totals.

Section 3

The Fix

The diagnostic tells you how unreliable your elasticities are. The fix reduces that unreliability, and it doesn't require changing your annual budget, your channel mix, or your campaign strategy.

The idea is simple: vary the weekly split between channels independently, while keeping monthly totals exactly the same. If TV is heavier in week one, let Meta be lighter, and vice versa the following week. Over time, the channels stop moving in lockstep. The MMM starts to see TV-high / Meta-low periods alongside TV-low / Meta-high periods, and can begin to distinguish their effects.

The mechanism

Within each calendar month, redistribute each channel's weekly spend randomly. One month, TV gets more budget in the first two weeks; the next month, the last two. Meta does the same independently. At the end of each month, rescale so the channel's monthly total is exactly preserved.

The media agency sees the same monthly booking. The annual budget is untouched. The only thing that changes is the within-month timing, which the business already adjusts informally for flights and promotional peaks. What's new is making that adjustment deliberately, independently, and consistently across all channels.

TV Meta

Left: original plan, TV and Meta follow the same weekly rhythm (channel correlation 0.68). Right: phased plan, weekly split varies independently per channel, monthly totals unchanged (correlation drops to ~0.2). The MMM can now observe TV-high / Meta-low weeks alongside TV-low / Meta-high weeks.

How much variation to introduce, and how we pick it

The amplitude of the phasing (how aggressively the weekly split is reshuffled) is the main dial. We choose it by grid search: testing a range of amplitudes, measuring the resulting CV at each, and recommending the amplitude that minimises the worst-case CV across channels, not the average, the worst case, so no single channel is left unreliable. More variation de-correlates channels faster and produces greater CV reduction; less variation is operationally easier but takes longer to show up in the model.

Our research shows this relationship is roughly linear: each additional 10 percentage points of weekly deviation produces approximately the same incremental improvement. There's no cliff edge or discontinuity, just a smooth trade-off between aggressiveness and benefit.

More weekly variation → greater CV reduction
Max elasticity band-width reduction (%) vs max weekly deviation from original plan · 52-week phased plan

At ±40% weekly deviation (a moderate level that most agencies can accommodate), phasing reduces maximum elasticity uncertainty by ~30% after one year (27–35% across 5 independent phasing draws). At ±80%, the reduction reaches ~53% (49–60% across the same 5 draws). The payoff is roughly linear, there is no magic threshold, just more de-correlation for more variation. The range reflects that "phasing" means one specific random reshuffle of the week-to-week mix; a different draw of the same amplitude gives a slightly different result, which is itself worth remembering before treating any single reduction number as exact.

What you get

The output of the phasing recommendation is a concrete 52-week spend schedule: one number per channel per week, with monthly totals guaranteed to match the original plan exactly. This is what you hand to the media agency, not a strategy document or a framework, but a booking grid.

In a real dataset (4 years of history, 1 year of plan, correlation 0.68), a ±37% weekly deviation produces a 16% reduction in maximum CV after one year of phasing (8–21% across 10 independent phasing draws). That sounds modest, and it is, because four years of correlated history dilutes one year of de-correlated plan data. The Impact section addresses this directly: how long does phasing need to run before the improvement becomes material?

Channel-specific constraints

Every result so far assumed every channel could move by the same amount. Real media plans rarely work that way: TV is often booked and contracted well ahead of time and simply can't be reshuffled week to week, while a channel like Search or paid social can flex much more freely. The package supports this directly, the weekly deviation allowance can be set per channel, a single number for a symmetric range, or, for a channel that genuinely can be turned off, a Blackout() setting that switches it fully on or fully off rather than nudging it up and down.

Locking a channel costs more than it looks like. If TV can't move at all, phasing still helps, Meta and Search de-correlate from each other and, to a lesser extent, from the now-fixed TV, but most of TV's own reliability gain disappears. In a worked example (three channels, correlation 0.7, ±40% deviation where allowed): phasing all three channels normally reduces worst-case CV by 16%; locking TV and phasing only Meta and Search reduces it by 12%, with TV's own CV barely moving. A channel only gets more reliable if it's actually allowed to vary.

Sometimes the honest constraint from an agency isn't "vary it a bit", it's "it's either running or it's off." Blackout() models that directly: instead of a continuous ± range, each week is either spent at the full planned amount or at exactly zero, nothing in between. Left uncapped, a bad month could land several dark weeks at once, forcing the few surviving weeks to carry an implausible multiple of the original budget. A max_dark_weeks_per_month cap bounds this, capped at one dark week a month, at most one week's spend ever needs a new home.

Because it concentrates variation into a genuine on/off switch rather than spreading it thinly, Blackout buys more reliability per unit of disruption than a continuous range at the same nominal deviation budget. Checked across three independent data draws (same 3-channel, correlation-0.7 scenario used throughout this guide):

What this isn't

Budget phasing is not A/B testing, and it's not a geo-experiment. You don't need a control group. You don't need to sacrifice efficiency in some markets to learn in others. The spend still goes where it was always going, the timing just varies more. The data design improvement is a by-product of normal media operations, not a separate experiment running in parallel.

Section 4

The Impact

Section 3 showed what to do. This section answers how big the benefit is, how long it takes, and whether it holds up at realistic channel counts.

The short answer: one year of phasing against four years of correlated history produces a modest improvement (roughly 16–30% CV reduction, depending on deviation amplitude, with some spread depending on which specific de-correlated schedule you happen to run). The benefit is real, but it takes time to compound. As phased periods accumulate, they displace correlated history and the model becomes progressively more reliable.

Time and aggressiveness are substitutes. ±80% deviation reaches ~53% CV reduction in one year. ±40% deviation needs roughly 2.5 years to reach the same level. If your planning horizon is short, use a higher deviation amplitude. If you have three or more years, moderate deviation is sufficient and operationally easier to maintain.

The grey dashed line oscillates (positive in some periods, negative in others) and only catches up to even the lightest phasing (±20% deviation) after five years. This is the key finding from our research: collinearity-driven unreliability doesn't fix itself quickly just by adding more observations. More data does help over the long run, but deliberate mix variation gets you a comparable benefit years sooner, and more predictably.

The improvement is monotonic but slowing. The biggest gains come in the first year. By year 2–3 the curve flattens, because the phased period is large enough to dominate the history and further accumulation produces diminishing returns. For most teams, year 1 delivers the most meaningful uplift relative to effort.

Does it scale?

Every result so far used a three-channel toy example: TV, Meta, Search. Real MMMs usually run more, paid social, paid search, display, OOH, radio, affiliates, and often more besides. Before trusting any of this on a real spend history, it's worth asking the obvious question: does the story hold up once you're past three channels?

We reran the diagnostic and the phasing recommendation at channel counts from 5 to 20, generic channels with randomly drawn elasticities (not hand-picked, to avoid stacking the deck), same demand-driven collinearity structure used throughout this guide.

All channels in this simulation share one latent demand signal, so more channels competing for that same signal could plausibly make individual effects harder to pin down. In practice it barely moves the needle. Holding correlation fixed at 0.7, mean band width across channels is essentially flat from 5 to 20 channels, and even the worst-case channel only widens modestly.

Mean band width moves from 0.349 (5 channels) to 0.355 (20 channels), a 1.9% change. The worst-case channel widens from 0.413 to 0.441, a 6.8% change, still small. This matches theory: for channels sharing one demand signal at fixed pairwise correlation, the per-channel variance inflation factor converges to a ceiling as channel count grows, rather than increasing without bound.

The practical takeaway: a 20-channel MMM is not meaningfully less reliable than a 3-channel one, by virtue of channel count alone. What matters is how correlated your channels are with each other (Section 1), not how many of them there are.

The more important question is whether the fix still works once there are more channels competing for the same phasing effort. It does, and the benefit doesn't decay with scale.

Next: Section 6 — Questions works through the practical pushback this guide tends to get: Bayesian methods, geo-testing, phasing's cost to performance, and hierarchical pooling.

Section 6

Questions

Four questions come up whenever this argument is presented to practitioners. Each deserves a direct answer rather than a dismissal.

Won't a Bayesian method fix it?

A common reaction: "we don't use plain OLS, we use a Bayesian MMM, priors regularise the estimates, so surely this isn't our problem." A prior is a statement about what you believed before seeing the data. In a Bayesian regression, it shrinks noisy estimates toward a plausible range and stabilises models that would otherwise overfit thin or noisy data, that's genuinely useful. But collinearity isn't a noisy-estimation problem in that sense: when TV and Meta spend move almost together, the likelihood is nearly flat along a ridge, dozens of very different (TV effect, Meta effect) pairs explain the data almost equally well, because the data never showed the model TV moving without Meta. A weakly-informative prior can pick a point to lean on within that ridge, but it doesn't add the missing information about which point is correct.

Priors regularise noise. They can't manufacture the independent variation that was never in the data to begin with.

We checked this directly: the highest-collinearity scenario from Section 1 (three channels, correlation 0.9, where OLS elasticity CV hits 60%), fit two ways, classical OLS with an analytic 95% confidence interval, and a Bayesian linear model in PyMC with deliberately weak, untuned priors. Averaged across ten independent noise draws (a single draw can itself be a lucky or unlucky one, the same phenomenon this guide is about, one level down):

Weakly-informative priors narrow the interval a little, not a lot
95% OLS confidence interval vs 95% Bayesian credible interval · same data, correlation = 0.9 · averaged over 10 noise draws
OLS 95% CI Bayesian 95% HDI

Mean interval width across 10 noise draws. TV: OLS 0.929 vs Bayes 0.862 (6.5% narrower). Meta: 0.984 vs 0.926 (5.2% narrower). Search: 1.639 vs 1.473 (9.1% narrower). Individual draws varied more, per-draw narrowing ranged from around 14% narrower to a slight ~3% widening in a few isolated draws for TV and Meta, a reminder that any single comparison is noisy; the mean across draws is the stable number. A real, modest regularisation effect, not a resolution of the underlying identification problem.

Roughly 5–9% tighter intervals is what weakly-informative priors bought here, not nothing, but nowhere close to fixing a problem that leaves OLS's own interval spanning most of the plausible range. This isn't an argument against Bayesian MMMs generally, tools like Meridian and Robyn earn their keep on a genuinely different problem, functional form (adstock decay, saturation curves), where the data does contain information the prior can usefully encode. Channel-level collinearity is a different failure mode: no amount of correctly-specified adstock or saturation curve fixes the fact that TV and Meta spend rose and fell together.

A strongly informative prior can produce a tight interval, but only if the prior itself is right, and the external knowledge to justify one (category benchmarks, a validated rate card) is exactly what's missing here. A confidently narrow interval built on an uncalibrated prior is worse than an honestly wide one: it looks precise enough to act on, and the budget decision built on it fails silently.

Why not just run a geo-lift test?

Geo-experiments (holding out matched control markets to measure incremental lift) are a genuine gold standard for causal identification, and where they're feasible, they should usually be preferred to any observational fix. But "just run a geo-test" undersells the practical cost, on four counts. It's impractical to run one for every channel simultaneously, TV, Meta and Search all at once, without the tests confounding each other. Each test produces one incrementality estimate for one channel in one period, not the ongoing, time-varying elasticities a budget allocator needs across the whole plan. Running one means genuinely holding spend back in control markets for the test window, a real, deliberate cost, not a hypothetical one. And it requires media buying and measurement infrastructure most teams don't have set up for continuous use.

Even where a geo-test is run, it isn't the clean fallback it's often presented as. Recast's June 2026 open-source benchmark study ran 32,000 simulated experiments across four widely-used geo-lift tools, CausalPy, Google Matched Markets, Meta GeoLift, and Google Causal Impact, under conditions practitioners actually encounter: an outlier test market, a small donor pool, and a short pre-treatment calibration window.

The headline finding: every tool trades off two failure modes, and none escapes the trade-off. Meta GeoLift keeps its 95% coverage promise and rarely false-alarms (3–5%), but its intervals are wide enough that it misses 89–96% of real effects, most tests simply come back inconclusive. Google Causal Impact sits at the other end: it detects a real 7.5% lift 52–66% of the time, but false-alarms nearly 30% of the time and carries a consistent positive bias. CausalPy and Google Matched Markets land in between, under-covering (76–86%) relative to their nominal 95%. And the conditions that make this worse, a short pre-treatment window, a small donor pool, an unusually large test market, are common, not edge cases: under a 30-day (rather than 90-day) pre-period, CausalPy and Causal Impact's false-positive rates both climb above 24%.

None of this means these tools are broken, they're doing exactly what their designers intended, and each is defensible depending on which mistake is more expensive for your business. The point is narrower: "just geo-test it" swaps one uncertainty problem (channel collinearity in an MMM) for another (calibration trade-offs in a geo-experiment tool), and doesn't scale to covering every channel in the plan. It's a genuinely useful complementary check, not a clean substitute.

Doesn't phasing hurt performance by moving spend away from the "right" moments?

Two different objections get bundled under this question, and they deserve different answers. The first is the media-planning folklore around dayparting and flighting: that there are optimal windows to spend in, and moving budget away from them destroys value. Much of that orthodoxy is promoted by the publishers selling the inventory it favours, with thin independent causal evidence behind the specific timing claims. We're not relying on it, and don't need to.

The second objection is real and checkable: if a channel's response to spend is concave (each additional pound does a little less than the last, the standard diminishing-returns shape), then spending unevenly across weeks has a genuine expected-value cost, by Jensen's inequality, the expected response to an uneven spend pattern is always less than or equal to the response to a smooth pattern spending the same total. Phasing deliberately introduces week-to-week unevenness, so this cost is real, not folklore.

We can check its size directly. The package doesn't yet fit a saturation curve (that's flagged as future work), so we illustrate the mechanism with a simple, clearly-stylised diminishing-returns curve, applied to the actual recommended phasing schedule from Section 3's real-dataset example, not a hypothetical one.

This is exactly the trade-off Jensen's inequality predicts, and it's small at the phasing amplitudes recommended in this guide (monthly totals preserved, weekly deviation capped at 40–80%). It's worth checking against your own fitted response curve once your MMM has one; if your channels saturate unusually sharply at the spend levels you're planning, the cost could run higher than shown here, and that's a reasonable input into how aggressive a phasing amplitude to choose.

Doesn't hierarchical pooling solve this?

In a panel MMM spanning multiple markets or countries, hierarchical (multilevel) models can partially pool channel effects across geographies, each market gets its own estimate, shrunk toward a shared group-level estimate, borrowing strength across markets to reduce the variance of any single one. It's a legitimate technique, and where the data supports it, it helps with a real problem.

It's just not a problem most readers of this guide have the data to solve. Most UK MMM practitioners run a single-market model, there's no second geography to pool across. Hierarchical pooling addresses cross-market variance; this guide addresses within-market identification variance from correlated channel spend, a different problem, and one that doesn't go away because a technique exists to solve a problem you don't have the data structure for.

Bringing it together

The six sections of this guide tell one story:

Marketing channels move together because business decisions are coordinated. That correlation doesn't bias your MMM, but it makes the elasticity estimates unreliable. The specific numbers your model produced are one draw from a wide distribution; a slightly different data window would give a different answer. The coefficient of variation quantifies exactly how wide that distribution is for your specific spend history.

The fix is to vary the weekly channel split independently within each month, preserving monthly totals exactly. This introduces the kind of natural variation that lets the model distinguish individual channel effects. The improvement compounds over time: the more phased periods accumulate in the training data, the more reliable the elasticity estimates become. And it isn't a toy-example artefact: the same benefit shows up whether your MMM has three channels or twenty.

This is not a complex statistical intervention. It's a deliberate data design choice: the same logic that motivates geo-experiments in paid search, applied to the temporal dimension of channel mix planning. Switching to a Bayesian estimation method doesn't sidestep this: priors regularise noise, they don't manufacture the independent variation the data never had. And the usual practical objections, run a geo-test instead, phasing must hurt performance, pool hierarchically, either don't hold up under scrutiny or don't apply to most practitioners' actual data.

And phasing isn't one-size-fits-all: it adapts to what an agency will actually accept, from a channel that's locked entirely to one that can be switched fully on or off. That harder lever, Blackout, turns out to be not just more flexible but more effective, bigger reliability gains, reached faster, holding up as channel count grows.

The question isn't whether to trust your MMM. It's whether your data gave it a fair chance. Budget phasing is how you give it one.
