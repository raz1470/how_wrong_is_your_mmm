How Wrong Is Your MMM?

Your model gives you one number, but the honest answer is a (very wide) range.

1 · The problem 2 · The cause 3 · The fix 4 · The impact 5 · How it works 6 · Questions

Here's an uncomfortable question. Take the marketing mix model you already trust, the one that split last year's sales across TV, Meta and Search. Now run it again: same channels, same market, just a slightly different slice of history. Would it give you the same answer?

For most brands, no. Here's why, and what to do about it.

1 · The problem

Your model is more unsure than it lets on

Take a real £12.3m annual plan: £5.1m on TV, £4.1m on Meta, £3.1m on Search. Ask the model for a single number and you'll get one. Ask it to be honest about the range of answers the same data could plausibly support, and the picture changes.

Same spend, same market, same true effect. This is the honest range once you account for how much the answer can genuinely move given how these channels were planned.

Look closely and none of these ranges are tight. TV could be earning anywhere from £945k to £2.09m off the same spend, more than double. Search's range is almost as wide in absolute terms, on barely a third of the budget. That's not a rounding error. That's the model telling you it genuinely doesn't know which channel is really carrying the plan.

2 · The cause

Your channels never move on their own

Here's why the model is so unsure. TV, Meta and Search budgets get set in the same planning cycle, so when one goes up, they usually all go up. Look at an actual planned year of spend below: the three lines rise and fall almost in lockstep.

Three channels, one planning cycle
Real planned weekly spend, 52 weeks
TV Meta Search

TV, Meta and Search spend move together nearly every week, an average correlation of 0.68. Your model has never actually seen TV move independently of the other two, so it can't confidently tell you how much of your sales are down to TV rather than Meta or Search.

It isn't that the model is badly built. It's that the data it's learning from was never designed to answer this question.

3 · The fix

Pace the same budget differently

You don't need a bigger model, a bigger budget, or an AI agent bolted onto your MMM. You need to move some of that same spend to different weeks within each month, so the channels stop moving in lockstep, and keep doing it. Every month still lands on the same total. So does every year. Here's what that looks like: the same annual and monthly totals, paced differently week to week.

The recommended schedule swings each channel's weekly spend by up to 80% either side of the original plan, repeated consistently for three years. Every month still adds up to exactly the same total, pound for pound.

4 · The impact

The least reliable number gets more reliable

We ran the model again, same starting point, but this time it had three years of consistently repaced spend behind it instead of none. Here's what moved.

Every channel's range narrows sharply: TV by 63%, Meta by 68%, Search by 75%, all off the same £12.3m a year, with no extra spend, just three years of consistently pacing it differently. 5 · How it works

One pipeline, three steps

All of this runs through how_wrong_is_your_mmm, a free, open-source python package. Point it at your own spend history and it runs the same three steps on your numbers, not a hypothetical example.

The diagnostic tells you how wide your true range is. Phasing gives you a spend schedule that narrows it, same budget, same totals. Retraining is where the payoff lands.

6 · Questions

What people ask first

Why not just run a geo-lift test instead?

Geo-experiments are the academic gold standard, but they test one channel at a time, need a real blackout region, and take months to read. You can't run one for TV, Meta and Search simultaneously. This works with the spend and channel mix you already have.

How long until I see the benefit?

It builds, it doesn't switch on overnight. At the pacing shown above, meaningful improvement shows up within the first year, and it keeps compounding the longer you stick with it: the 75% figure here is what three consistent years buys you, not week one.

What do I actually hand my media agency?

A weekly spend number per channel, covering the same plan year. Every month still totals exactly what it does today. Nothing about the buy itself changes, only which week the money lands in.
