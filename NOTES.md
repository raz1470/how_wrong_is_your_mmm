## Goal

A Python package that diagnoses collinearity in Marketing Mix Model spend data, quantifies how much it biases elasticity estimates, and recommends budget perturbations to fix it â€” with Bayesian uncertainty throughout.

**PyPI eventually.** Author: Ryan O'Sullivan.

## The problem this solves

Real marketing data is generated by business decisions, not experiments. TV and digital budgets move together because the same planning cycle controls both. This means an MMM can't reliably distinguish individual channel effects â€” the elasticities are biased, and the direction of bias is unknown without simulation.

Most practitioners either ignore collinearity or run a VIF check and shrug. Neither tells them *how wrong* the elasticities actually are, or what to *do* about it.

## The three-part solution

**Part 1 â€” DGP simulation (prove the problem)**
Generate synthetic correlated spend data with *known* true elasticities. Fit an MMM. Show the estimated elasticities are wrong. Quantify the bias as a function of the correlation structure. This is the diagnostic: "here is how wrong your MMM probably is, given the collinearity in your data."

**Part 2 â€” Budget phasing recommendation (design the fix)**
Given the current correlation structure of spend, recommend future budget phasing that deliberately de-correlates channels week-to-week. The goal is to introduce independent variation that lets the MMM distinguish individual channel effects. This is the experiment-design step â€” the equivalent of Vaver & Koehler's geo-experiments but applied to channel mix rather than geography.

**Part 3 â€” Sampling weights (speed up the benefit)**
Upweight recent de-correlated observations in the MMM so the model benefits quickly without waiting years for the new data to dominate the history. Equivalent to a form of importance sampling â€” we give more weight to informative (low-collinearity) periods.

## Positioning

This is in the same spirit as the Vaver & Koehler geo-experiment literature from Google: using deliberate variation to identify causal effects. The difference is:
- **Their instrument:** geographic variation
- **Our instrument:** channel mix variation over time
- **Our twist:** Bayesian uncertainty on both the diagnosis and the recommendation

The collinearity/budget adjuster idea is strong enough to be an **academic paper**, not just a package. Could drive significant LinkedIn traction in the MMM / causal inference community.

## Key open questions (to resolve during build)

- **What collinearity metric drives the budget recommendation?** VIF is intuitive but has known pathologies at high K. Condition number of the design matrix is more stable. Could offer both.
- **How to define "budget perturbation" formally?** Constrained optimisation: minimise condition number (or max VIF) subject to total budget = constant and per-channel bounds. `scipy.optimize` is the natural tool; a Bayesian version would put uncertainty on the channel response curves first.
- **Sampling weights scheme?** Inverse-VIF weighting per observation period? Or model-based: fit a time-varying collinearity measure (rolling condition number) and use it as the weight?
- **Paper angle?** Simulation study showing coefficient instability as a function of correlation strength, sample size, and number of channels. Propose the perturbation + weighting scheme. Validate on synthetic and (anonymised) real data.

## Core pipeline design (locked in session 2)

One pipeline, two entry points â€” everything from synthetic sales onwards is identical:

**Entry point A â€” fully synthetic:**
Generate synthetic spend (demand-driven DGP) â†’ create synthetic sales using known elasticities â†’ fit OLS â†’ compare estimated to true

**Entry point B â€” real spend:**
User supplies actual spend DataFrame â†’ create synthetic sales using known elasticities applied to real spend â†’ fit OLS â†’ compare estimated to true

The key insight: we never simulate spend when real data is available. We simulate only the sales column (using known elasticities Ã— real spend + noise). The collinearity in the real spend is already baked into the data â€” no need to model it.

**Why this is powerful:** the diagnostic is personalised. You're not showing a generic collinearity problem, you're showing *their* problem with *their* spend data. "Given how your channels actually moved over the last 2 years, here's how unreliable your MMM elasticities are."

**The "how wrong" number:** coefficient of variation of the elasticity estimate across many noise seeds. Not wrong vs unknowable truth, but "how much would your answer change if you'd measured in a slightly different period?"

## What "wrong" means in this package (locked in session 2)

Standard OLS collinearity does not cause **bias** â€” it causes **variance**. OLS estimates are still centred on the truth even with correlated predictors (as long as the model is correctly specified). The problem is **unreliability**: the estimates swing wildly across different realisations of the data.

This maps directly to what practitioners experience: "we refit with 3 more months of data and TV elasticity halved." The model isn't broken â€” the data is uninformative. Collinearity means you can't trust the number you got, because a slightly different dataset would give a very different number.

The diagnostic quantifies this unreliability. The budget perturbation reduces it.

## BudgetPhaser design (locked session 4)

The phasing mechanism: for each month, redistribute each channel's weekly spend randomly within the month, then rescale to preserve the monthly total exactly. This introduces independent weekly variation across channels without touching monthly or annual commitments.

Grid search over amplitude Î± âˆˆ [0, 1]. Î±=0 = no change. Î±=1 = maximum allowed weekly deviation (default 40% per channel). Recommended Î± = argmin(max CV across channels).

Output to practitioner: a concrete 52Ã—N weekly spend schedule to hand to the media agency, with monthly totals unchanged.

## Architecture change needed for Part 3 (agreed session 4)

**The data reality:** MMMs are fit on 3-5 years of weekly data, not just the plan year. The practitioner only controls next year's spend. The rest is history â€” correlated, fixed, out of their hands.

**Implication for CollinearityWeighter:** it should operate on the full historical dataset (3-5 years) with sampling weights that upweight the latest year. The phased plan is only applied to the latest year. Collinearity in the history is accepted; the weighter and phasing together make the latest year do more work in the model fit.

**Revised architecture:**
- `CollinearityDiagnostic` and `BudgetPhaser` accept a full multi-year spend DataFrame with a DatetimeIndex.
- A `plan_year` parameter (or the last 52 weeks by default) identifies which period is adjustable.
- `CollinearityWeighter` takes the full spend + weights and upweights recent de-correlated observations.
- The diagnostic is evaluated against the weighted full dataset, not just the plan year.

This is the design to implement in Part 3. Don't build `CollinearityWeighter` without this framing â€” the 52-week-only design would understate the benefit of phasing (history dilutes it) and overstate how much control the practitioner has.

## Session learnings (session 2)

- **Revenue noise must be substantial relative to channel signal.** With noise=50 and channel contributions ~Â£70k, OLS recovers coefficients near-perfectly regardless of collinearity. Set noise to Â£20k (same order of magnitude as channel contributions) to make unreliability visible. In general: if the story isn't showing up, check the SNR first.
- **The `_noise_std_from_correlation` derivation.** If TV = demand + noise, Meta = demand + noise (all N(0,1)), then Corr(TV, Meta) = 1/(1 + sigmaÂ²). Solve: sigma = sqrt((1-corr)/corr). This gives target correlation â‰ˆ actual correlation for large n_obs but diverges at small samples â€” the actual correlation reported by `actual_correlation` is what matters for the notebook.
- **Actual correlation â‰  target correlation at n_obs=104.** The target is a population parameter; the actual is a sample statistic. Always report actual in the notebook, not target.
